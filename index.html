Introduction

Your brain doesn't
care about your prime.

Ever wanted to be at your prime, doing everything you wanted to, getting all the recognition and feeling euphoric? Your own brain doesn't give a damn about any of that. All it will do is ensure your survival.

You could have autism, or ADHD and take medicine to ease the symptoms — but your own brain will try to counteract the drug, even if it helps.

The real stakes

The brain's neurochemistry isn't as abstract as you think it is. If you want to get recognition, if you want to have presence, if you want to be at your maximum peak — it all breaks down to neuroscience. The perception of other people, the bonding and reward from human connection, the confidence you feel — all of it originates in the brain.

To all those doing this mostly for the credits and the recognition: if you knew neuroscience well enough like you claim to, you would know no one cares about that. This has a larger-than-life meaning — where you discover yourself, where you realize why you do things, where you understand what's happening inside you as you read this right now.

Don't want to feel sad? Your brain is going to do what it thinks is right regardless. Unless you understand the machinery of your own brain, you won't be able to work with it. Once you can, you will reach a level you once thought was impossible. This isn't about showing how smart you are. It's about self-discovery.

Real world — what tolerance looks like

Go downtown to Kensington, Philadelphia (which I recommend you don't), or try NYC, and you will see stimulant and opioid addicts nodding their heads on the street, losing their own lives for a drug. Children of addicts who didn't feel raised by a parent — they felt raised by that drug. Why does this happen? How can such things make you feel like you're burning in hell, facing severe anxiety, going through opioid withdrawal? That's the connection I will be making here.

Real world — memory and trauma

Take survivors of extreme trauma who couldn't remember the events they faced. They didn't simply forget — it's never that simple. Their brain was hiding it from them. No matter how much you beg your brain to tell you what happened, it will refuse. That's not weakness. That's a survival mechanism running exactly as designed.

Your enemies are just pawns the brain uses to checkmate you. We think people hurt us — but often it's our own mind replaying and amplifying the event that actually keeps us stuck.
